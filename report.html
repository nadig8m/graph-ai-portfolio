Child Mortality and Primary Education Access in 2020

Overview

This report compares two Gapminder indicators for 2020:

  • Child mortality (0–5 year olds dying per 1,000 born)
  • Children out of school (primary)

Both datasets are subset to 2020, mapped globally, and compared in a scatterplot.

Child mortality in 2020

In 2020, 194 countries and territories have child mortality data. Values range from 1.9 to 114.4 deaths per 1,000 live births.

Children out of school (primary) in 2020

In 2020, 141 countries report counts of primary-age children out of school. Reported values range from 11 to 3,989,763 children.

Interpretation

Across countries with complete 2020 data, child mortality and the number of primary-age children out of school show a moderate positive association (Pearson r = 0.29). Countries with higher under-5 mortality tend to report more children out of school, but the relationship is far from perfect.

Several patterns help explain this:

  • Shared drivers of disadvantage. Higher child mortality and larger out-of-school populations both reflect broader constraints—poverty, weak health systems, and limited education infrastructure—that often occur together in the same countries, especially in parts of sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia visible on the maps.
  • Population size matters for counts. The out-of-school indicator is an absolute count, not a rate. Large countries such as India can appear high on the y-axis even when mortality is moderate, which adds scatter and weakens a simple linear fit (slope ≈ 5,827 additional children out of school per one-unit increase in mortality; p < 0.001).
  • Many high-income countries cluster differently. Several countries combine low mortality with relatively small out-of-school counts, while outliers with high mortality but moderate counts suggest that education access can diverge from health outcomes within the same mortality band.

Overall, the maps and scatterplot suggest that health and primary-school access are related dimensions of child well-being, but they are not interchangeable: reducing mortality alone would not automatically eliminate out-of-school children, and vice versa.
